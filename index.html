About the award

The programme

What is the Arts Award?

Arts Award is a real qualification, awarded by Trinity College London, and the best part is that you choose what it's about. There's no exam.

It runs at several levels, and you begin with Bronze. Later, as you move up the school, you can carry on to Silver and then Gold, taking on bigger projects of your own and helping to lead others as you go.

For Bronze, you take part in an art form you care about, go and experience art made by other people, find an artist who inspires you, and pass a skill of your own on to someone else. You keep a record as you go, and at the end you have a portfolio you built yourself.

The namesake

Who was Thomas Tallis?

The school is named after Thomas Tallis, a composer who lived in Greenwich roughly five hundred years ago, from about 1505 to 1585. The school likes to call him a true original, and it's hard to argue: he made music for the kings and queens of the sixteenth century, and some of it is still sung today.

He was also, in his way, an entrepreneur. In 1575 he and a younger composer, William Byrd, were granted the first patent ever given in England to print and publish music, with a monopoly on polyphonic music, the kind where many voices weave at once. The school has a nice way of putting it: a bit like one band being handed the only licence in the country to make pop.

His boldest piece, Spem in alium, was written for Elizabeth I and needs forty separate singers, each holding their own line. Four hundred years on, in 2012, it climbed back to number one in the classical charts. He could write small and plain too: If Ye Love Me, only a few lines long, is still loved for the opposite reason.

What makes him a fitting namesake is how he managed all of it. Tallis served four monarchs in a row, at a time when each new king or queen swung the country between Catholic and Protestant, with real danger for anyone caught on the wrong side.

Tallis kept his head, kept his post, and made music for everyone. When Henry VIII broke with the Catholic church in 1534, Tallis quietly changed how he wrote, leaning less on showy flourishes and more on rhythm and the fit between words and music. He borrowed ideas arriving from across Europe, moved between styles, and carried on composing until he died.

He spent his last years in Greenwich and is buried at St Alfege's church, which is why every Year 7 goes to see it. Nearby, where the Old Royal Naval College now stands, there was once a royal palace, and three of the four monarchs he served were born on that very spot.

Did you know? The three waves in the school's logo aren't only decoration. A designer built them from the shape of the real sound waves made by a voice saying "Thomas Tallis School". Tallis worked in sound, so the school quietly wears his medium: sound, drawn as a line.
